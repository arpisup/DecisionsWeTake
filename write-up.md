# Design Rationale — The Daily Reflection Tree

## Why These Questions

### The Opening: "Describe today in one word"

The opening question isn't diagnostic — it's an emotional doorway. A single-word framing forces the employee to *commit* to a felt sense of their day before analysis begins. The four options (Productive, Tough, Mixed, Draining) aren't random; they're arranged along an affective axis from positive-agentic to negative-passive. This initial answer sets the tone and routes the employee into an appropriate follow-up — someone who says "Productive" needs a different first question than someone who says "Draining."

I deliberately chose **four options, not two**, to avoid a false binary. "Mixed" is the honest middle ground most people live in. "Draining" is distinct from "Tough" — toughness implies resistance encountered; draining implies energy depleted. That nuance matters for how the tree addresses them.

### Axis 1: Locus of Control

I designed two parallel tracks — **AGENCY_HIGH** (for Productive/Mixed) and **AGENCY_LOW** (for Tough/Draining) — asking the same underlying question ("Where do you locate causality?") but framed for the employee's emotional context. Asking a drained person "What made things go well?" feels tone-deaf. Asking a productive person "Where did your mind go when things got hard?" wastes the positive framing.

The follow-up questions (**SETBACK** for internally-leaning respondents, **CHOICE** for externally-leaning) are designed to *test the initial signal*. An employee who said things went well because "I adapted" (internal) is then asked about a setback — do they maintain that internal frame, or does it collapse? Conversely, someone leaning external is asked to *find* a moment of choice — not to correct them, but to see if agency was present underneath the frustration.

**Source:** Rotter's (1954) Internal-External Locus of Control scale guided the option design. Each question has exactly 2 internal and 2 external options, balanced to avoid leading. Dweck's growth mindset research (2006) informed the reflection texts — particularly the idea that noticing agency is itself a skill that can develop.

### Axis 2: Orientation

The opening question ("One interaction — giving or expecting?") works because contribution and entitlement are almost always invisible in the moment. You don't *think* "I'm being entitled" — you think "I'm being undervalued." The options make this visible without labeling it.

The **mirror questions** are the design's most deliberate feature. After two questions establish a signal, the tree *flips the mirror*:
- Contributors are asked: "Did you wish someone would give to *you*?" — surfacing the hidden need beneath the giving
- Entitled-leaning respondents are asked: "Was there a moment you gave freely?" — surfacing the contribution they may have overlooked

This creates a moment of cognitive surprise. The tree doesn't moralize; it reveals a truth the employee already knows but hasn't articulated.

**Source:** Campbell et al.'s (2004) Psychological Entitlement Scale and Organ's (1988) OCB framework. The mirror technique draws from motivational interviewing — reflecting back a discrepancy the person can resolve themselves.

### Axis 3: Radius of Concern

The progression from "Who comes to mind?" to the forward-looking "Tomorrow" question is designed to move from observation to intention. The first question surfaces the current radius. The second explores it. The third points it toward action — creating a micro-commitment for the next day.

The **TOMORROW_IN** variant (for self-focused employees) is carefully worded: "Tomorrow doesn't need to be a transformation — just a slight shift." This avoids the trap of making the employee feel they're failing by being self-focused. It normalizes the current state while gently suggesting a next step.

**Source:** Maslow's (1969) self-transcendence theory — the idea that the healthiest orientation is outward. Batson's (2011) perspective-taking research informed the options — particularly the distinction between awareness ("I noticed") and action ("I checked in").

## Branching Design & Trade-offs

### The Three-Decision Structure Per Axis

Each axis follows the same skeleton: **Open → Route → Deepen → Route → Mirror/Extend → Route → Reflect**. Three question-decision cycles per axis provide enough signal for a meaningful tally (3 data points each) without making the session feel like a survey.

**Trade-off:** More questions per axis would increase diagnostic accuracy but decrease engagement. At 7pm, after a full day, the employee's tolerance is ~5 minutes. 9 questions across 3 axes, with reflections and bridges, hits that window. I chose depth-per-question over breadth.

### Convergent Reflections

Each axis converges to three possible reflections (positive, negative, mixed). I considered more granular outcomes (6 per axis for 216 total endings) but decided **3 per axis is the right grain**. The reflections are written to be meaningful whether the tally is 3-0 or 2-1. The "mixed" reflection acknowledges ambiguity rather than forcing a binary reading.

**Trade-off:** Fewer endings mean less personalization. But each reflection is carefully crafted — a generic but well-written reflection beats a hyper-specific but shallow one.

### Bridges as Emotional Architecture

The bridges between axes aren't just "Moving on." They reframe: "From how you handled things → to what you gave" and "From what you gave → to who was in the picture." This creates a narrative arc: **agency → generosity → transcendence**. Each bridge takes what was learned in the previous axis and connects it to the next.

## Tone

The tree never judges. Someone who answers every question on the "negative" pole (external, entitled, self-centric) receives:
- Validation ("That's human, especially on a hard day")
- A gentle reframe ("But look again...")
- A concrete observation ("Somewhere today, you made a call")

The closing message for this persona is labeled "compassionate" — not "needs improvement." The tree is a mirror, not a scorecard.

## What I'd Improve With More Time

1. **Temporal adaptation.** A tree that remembers yesterday's answers and adjusts today's opening: "Yesterday you said 'Draining.' How about today?" This creates longitudinal self-awareness.

2. **Option ordering.** Currently, options are fixed. Randomizing option order per session would reduce anchoring bias (people tend to pick earlier options).

3. **Deeper branching on Axis 3.** The radius axis has less depth than Axes 1 and 2. With more time, I'd add a question about *specific* others — "Who specifically came to mind?" with role-based options (colleague, manager, report, customer) — to make the radius tangible.

4. **Micro-commitments with recall.** The "Tomorrow" questions create intentions. A production system would surface these the next evening: "Yesterday you said you'd check in with someone. Did you?"

5. **Edge case handling.** The current tree assumes the employee had interactions with others. A remote worker who spent the day solo might find Axis 2 questions awkward. Adding a "worked alone today" branch would improve validity.
